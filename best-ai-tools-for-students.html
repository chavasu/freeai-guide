Academic Integrity
Using AI responsibly — what universities actually allow
This matters. Read this section before submitting any AI-assisted work.

AI tools are increasingly permitted in academic settings — but the rules vary significantly by institution, department, and even individual professor. Never assume AI is permitted without checking your specific assignment brief and module handbook.

The most important distinction most universities make is between AI as a learning tool and AI as a submission tool. Using ChatGPT to understand a concept, check your grammar, or get feedback on your own draft is widely accepted. Submitting AI-generated text as your own work without disclosure is academic misconduct at virtually every institution — even where AI is otherwise permitted.

⚠️
On AI detection: As of 2026, most universities use Turnitin's AI module or GPTZero to screen submissions. These tools are not perfect — they carry a false positive rate between 1–10% — but they consistently flag heavily AI-generated text. Students who use AI to generate full submissions and submit unchanged are routinely caught. The evidence is in the writing: AI-generated prose has identifiable patterns that experienced academics recognise even without tools.

The ethical way to use AI in your studies: Use it to understand, not to replace. Ask Claude to explain a concept you didn't grasp from the lecture. Use Perplexity to find sources you can read and cite yourself. Use Grammarly to clean up grammar in an essay you wrote. Use Wolfram Alpha to check your maths working. These uses build skill. Submitting AI output as your work does not — and the skill gap becomes apparent in exams, vivas, and eventually in your career.

📋
Best practice: If your assignment allows AI tools, disclose how you used them — even briefly. A sentence like "I used Grammarly to proofread this essay and ChatGPT to generate initial outlines which I then significantly revised" demonstrates academic transparency and is increasingly expected at forward-thinking institutions.
FAQ
Common questions students ask about AI tools
What is the best free AI tool for students in 2026?
For most students, the best single free tool is Google Gemini — especially if you have a .edu email that qualifies you for up to 12 months of Gemini AI Pro free. It works inside Google Docs, has a 1M token context window, and integrates with all Google Workspace tools most students already use. If you don't qualify for that deal, the combination of Claude free (for writing) + Perplexity free (for research) + NotebookLM (for note-taking) covers nearly everything at zero cost.
Is using AI for homework cheating?
It depends on how you use it and what your institution's policy says. Using AI to understand concepts, check grammar, find research sources, or get feedback on your own writing is generally permitted and is not cheating. Submitting AI-generated text as your own work without disclosure — at most institutions — is academic misconduct. Always check your specific assignment brief. When in doubt, ask your lecturer directly.
Which AI is best for writing essays?
Claude (free tier) produces the most natural, academic-quality prose of any AI tool available. It's the consistent choice among professional writers and academics for long-form work. For writing support, use Claude to get feedback on your own draft, help with structure, or explain how to improve a specific paragraph. ChatGPT is a strong second option with more features on the free tier (including image generation).
What is the best AI for research papers and citations?
Perplexity AI is the best tool for cited research — every answer includes numbered, clickable source links. For working with papers you've already found, NotebookLM is outstanding: upload the PDFs and ask questions about them. For citation formatting specifically (APA, MLA, Chicago), QuillBot's citation generator or free tools like Zotero handle this reliably. Never rely on ChatGPT or Claude alone for citations — they can hallucinate references that don't exist.
Is ChatGPT free for students?
Yes — ChatGPT has a genuinely useful free tier that includes GPT-5.2 access, limited image generation, and web search. However, the free tier has daily message limits. Unlike Gemini and GitHub Copilot, OpenAI does not currently offer a student-specific discount or extended free plan. If you need more than the free tier offers, the $20/month Plus plan is the next step up.
Can AI help with maths and science subjects?
Yes — and this is one of the strongest use cases. Wolfram Alpha solves virtually any maths problem step-by-step (free answer, paid steps). Photomath scans handwritten problems with your phone camera. DeepSeek achieves 97.3% on MATH-500 (tied with ChatGPT) and shows its full reasoning for free. For understanding dense scientific papers, SciSpace explains complex methodologies in plain language. For coding assignments, GitHub Copilot is free with a student .edu email.
How do I get AI Pro free as a student?
Three main routes: (1) Google AI Pro — go to the Google student offer page and verify with your .edu email through SheerID. In eligible regions, this gives up to 12 months of Gemini 3.1 Pro free (worth $20/month). (2) GitHub Student Developer Pack — go to education.github.com, verify with your student email, and get GitHub Copilot plus dozens of other developer tools free. (3) Notion Plus — go to notion.so/students and apply with your student email for free Pro access.
Is NotebookLM free?
Yes — NotebookLM is completely free with a Google account. There is no paid tier for most users. You can upload PDFs, lecture slides, audio files, and text documents, then ask questions about them, generate study guides, create flashcards, and produce AI audio summaries. It is one of the most genuinely useful free tools for students in 2026 and requires no subscription whatsoever.
What AI tools do universities use to detect AI writing?
As of 2026, the most widely used tools are Turnitin's AI detection module (now integrated into most institutional Turnitin subscriptions) and GPTZero. Both flag AI-generated content with reasonable accuracy but carry a false positive rate of 1–10% depending on writing style. Heavily AI-generated text is consistently flagged. Text that has been substantially rewritten in your own voice is harder to detect, though experienced academics often recognise AI patterns without tools. Academic integrity remains the stronger reason to use AI as a learning tool rather than a submission tool.
